Change brief rule

Do not announce “AI is rolling out.” Announce the workflow change.

People need to know what task changes, what stays human-reviewed, where to get help, and how to pause or escalate. Keep the brief generic and sanitized: do not paste customer data, employee records, vendor contracts, production credentials, account screenshots, internal strategy, raw tickets, regulated records, or private repository links into a shared adoption note.

Brief flow

Answer six adoption questions

1. What workflow is changing?

Name the repeated task, current pain, pilot evidence, and the narrow version of the AI-assisted step. Avoid broad labels like “AI transformation.”

2. Who is affected first?

List the smallest group that will see the change: reviewers, operators, managers, support partners, security partners, or a pilot user group.

3. What stays human-owned?

State the human review gate, accountable owner, escalation route, and decisions the AI workflow is not allowed to make.

4. What support do people get?

Name the owner, office-hours window, quick reference, feedback channel, and the evidence users should report when something fails.

5. What is the reversible boundary?

Define allowed users, allowed inputs, rollback trigger, pause authority, and the next review date before any wider rollout.

6. What message will stakeholders hear?

Write one plain-language update that explains the workflow change, why it is limited, what proof exists, and what happens next.

Adoption risks

Catch these before rollout

Role confusion

“Am I still responsible?”

Point to the workflow owner card. The adoption note should say exactly who reviews, approves, supports, pauses, and improves the workflow.

Shadow process

“People will use it differently.”

Limit the first use case and allowed inputs. Ask users to report deviations before the process becomes unofficially broader than the pilot.

Support gap

“Who helps when it breaks?”

Name the support route and failure evidence to collect. If no support owner exists, the workflow is not ready to expand.

Trust gap

“Why should we believe this?”

Use the evidence log and stakeholder concern resolver. Share sanitized proof, known limits, and the next review date instead of hype.

Training gap

“What do users need to learn?”

Keep training to one job aid: what to do, what not to paste, how to check output, and when to escalate to a human.

Rollback gap

“How do we stop safely?”

Use the rollout checklist and sunset/archive checklist to name pause triggers, access cleanup, notification, and evidence retention.

Copyable adoption note

Paste this into a rollout or manager update

Workflow change: [one repeated task and the narrow AI-assisted step]

Why now: [sanitized pilot evidence, friction reduced, or decision need]

Who is included: [first user group, reviewers, owner, support route]

Human-owned boundary: [review gate, decisions AI cannot make, escalation rule]

Allowed inputs: [safe input categories only]

Support and feedback: [where users ask for help and what evidence they should capture]

Pause trigger: [quality, safety, support, adoption, or policy signal that stops expansion]

Next review: [date, forum, and decision: continue / narrow / pause / expand carefully]

Facilitator cue

When to use it in the workshop

After pilot evidence

Bridge proof to adoption

Use the brief after the evidence log or decision memo says continue, but before the team treats the workflow as normal practice.

Before rollout readiness

Make readiness human-readable

Turn rollout checklist answers into a stakeholder-facing note that explains who is affected and how the change stays reversible.

When resistance appears

Separate objection from confusion

If people push back, decide whether the issue is missing evidence, unclear ownership, weak support, or a real stop condition.
