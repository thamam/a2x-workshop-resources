Cadence rule

Govern by rhythm, evidence, and stop rules — not enthusiasm.

A steering cadence is a lightweight recurring forum for the few people who can keep an AI workflow useful, safe, and reversible. Keep it sanitized: do not paste customer data, employee records, contracts, pricing promises, production credentials, account screenshots, internal strategy, raw tickets, regulated records, or private repository links into shared cadence notes.

Cadence setup

Answer seven steering questions

1. What decisions belong here?

Choose the recurring decisions: continue, clarify, narrow, expand, pause, stop, change guardrails, assign owner time, or escalate a risk.

2. Who must attend?

Name the accountable owner, human reviewer, sponsor or delegate, security/privacy partner when needed, support owner, and one decision recorder.

3. What evidence must arrive?

Bring sanitized usage counts, reviewer decisions, quality signals, incidents, support friction, value evidence, open risks, and owner capacity signals.

4. How often should it meet?

Set a short initial rhythm: weekly during trial, biweekly during controlled rollout, monthly during stable operations, and immediate review after incidents.

5. What thresholds trigger action?

Define pause triggers, escalation triggers, rollback conditions, scope-expansion requirements, and what evidence is not strong enough to continue.

6. What artifacts get updated?

Decide which notes, owner card, data boundary, risk register, stakeholder update, health review, and sunset/archive packet must change after each meeting.

7. What happens after the meeting?

End with one state, one owner, one due date, and one next evidence requirement. If nothing changes, explicitly say the current boundary stays in force.

Cadence traps

Prevent these before the forum becomes theater

Too many people

A status meeting, not steering.

Invite observers only when needed. The standing cadence needs decision owners, reviewers, and support partners — not an audience.

No evidence packet

Opinions fill the gap.

Require a short sanitized packet before the meeting: evidence log, health review, risk changes, and the decision needed.

No stop rule

Momentum becomes default approval.

Name pause, rollback, and archive conditions before a workflow spreads. A cadence without stop rules only ratifies drift.

Owner blur

Everyone agrees; nobody owns.

Each action needs one owner and date. Use the workflow owner card if ownership is unclear.

Private evidence leak

Screenshots sneak into notes.

Record categories, counts, decisions, and sanitized examples. Keep raw prompts, tickets, logs, customer records, and account screens out.

Cadence never changes

The rhythm outlives the risk.

Review the cadence itself. Tighten it after incidents, loosen it when stable, and archive it when the workflow closes.

Copyable cadence note

Paste this into a steering agenda

Decision needed: [continue / clarify / narrow / expand / pause / stop / change guardrail / escalate risk]

Workflow boundary: [allowed users, allowed inputs, off-limits data, review gate, rollback trigger]

Evidence since last review: [sanitized usage, value, quality, reviewer decisions, incidents, support friction, owner capacity]

Open risks or concerns: [risk category, current mitigation, owner, threshold for action]

Artifacts to update: [owner card, data boundary, risk register, stakeholder update, health review, sunset/archive notes]

Decision and owner: [chosen state, accountable owner, due date, next evidence requirement]

Next cadence: [date, attendees, evidence packet, and condition that would trigger an earlier review]

When to use it in the workshop

After sponsor alignment

Turn attention into operating rhythm

If the sponsor approves a narrow next step, use this to decide who watches the pilot and what evidence returns.

During controlled rollout

Keep expansion reversible

Use the cadence before more teams or data types enter the workflow so expansion has thresholds and rollback signals.

After operations handoff

Prevent silent drift

Use this when an AI workflow becomes routine and needs periodic review without turning every update into a major meeting.
