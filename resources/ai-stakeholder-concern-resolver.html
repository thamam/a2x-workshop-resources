Concern resolver rule

Do not dismiss the concern. Translate it into a test.

A useful AI objection usually points to missing evidence, unclear ownership, weak boundaries, or a policy gap. Capture the concern generically, decide what would prove it safe enough to continue, and keep the next step small. Do not paste private customer data, employee records, contracts, credentials, production logs, screenshots, internal strategy, or private repository links into a shared concern note.

Resolver flow

Work through five questions

1. What is the concern really about?

Name the category: data exposure, accuracy, user adoption, ownership, cost risk, compliance, support burden, lock-in, rollout pressure, or reputational harm.

2. What boundary would make it safer?

Define what the pilot will not touch yet: production actions, customer records, regulated data, irreversible changes, external sending, private repositories, or broad user rollout.

3. What evidence would change the conversation?

Ask for a sanitized sample test, reviewer pass/fail signal, adoption observation, failure example, support note, policy answer, or risk-register row.

4. Who owns the answer?

Name the person or role who can provide the proof, approve the boundary, review the output, answer the policy question, or decide that the pilot should pause.

5. What is the smallest reversible next step?

Choose one next move: clarify, collect evidence, narrow scope, add a guardrail, run a sanitized test, schedule a decision meeting, or stop.

Concern patterns

Map the objection to the next artifact

Data concern

“What data is this touching?”

Open the data boundary checklist. The next step is to define allowed inputs, redaction, retention, cleanup, reviewer access, and escalation before evidence collection.

Risk concern

“What can go wrong?”

Open the risk register. The next step is one row with risk category, mitigation, owner, review signal, and stop condition.

Evidence concern

“How do we know this works?”

Open the evidence log or decision memo. The next step is a sanitized proof packet with observed value, friction, reviewer decisions, and open questions.

Ownership concern

“Who is accountable?”

Open the workflow owner card. The next step is to name the owner, backup, reviewer, support route, escalation boundary, and pause authority.

Rollout concern

“Are we moving too fast?”

Open the rollout readiness checklist. The next step is to confirm users, allowed inputs, review gates, rollback path, and communication before expansion.

Trust concern

“Stakeholders are not aligned.”

Open the decision meeting agenda. The next step is a short meeting that chooses continue, clarify, expand narrowly, pause, stop, or archive.

Copyable resolver note

Paste this into meeting notes

Concern: [generic concern category and one-sentence concern]

Why it matters: [decision, risk, user, policy, ownership, support, or trust reason]

Current boundary: [what the pilot will not touch until resolved]

Evidence needed: [sanitized test, reviewer signal, policy answer, risk row, adoption observation, or support note]

Owner: [role or person responsible for answering]

Next step: [clarify / collect evidence / narrow scope / add guardrail / decide / stop]

Review date: [date and forum where the concern will be revisited]

Facilitator cue

When to use it in the workshop

During Q&A

Honor skeptical questions

Use the resolver when a concern appears too broad to answer live. Convert it into a worksheet row and route it to the right follow-up artifact.

Before decision meetings

Prevent vague debate

Ask each stakeholder to bring one concern row with a boundary and evidence request before the decision meeting starts.

After updates

Close the loop

When a stakeholder update triggers objections, use this page to decide whether the pilot needs more evidence, narrower scope, a named owner, or a pause.
