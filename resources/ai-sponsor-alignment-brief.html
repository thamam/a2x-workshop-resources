Sponsor rule

Ask for one decision, not vague executive support.

A sponsor brief should name the workflow, the proof so far, the next funding or attention gate, the owner, and the reversible boundary. Keep it sanitized: do not paste customer data, employee records, contracts, pricing promises, production credentials, account screenshots, internal strategy, raw tickets, regulated records, or private repository links into a shared sponsor packet.

Brief flow

Answer seven sponsor questions

1. What decision do we need?

Pick one ask: continue the pilot, fund a narrow rollout, assign owner time, approve a data boundary, unblock a vendor trial, or stop cleanly.

2. What workflow is being sponsored?

Name the repeated task, current pain, target users, and the narrow AI-assisted step. Avoid broad “AI initiative” language.

3. What evidence exists?

Summarize sanitized pilot evidence: observed runs, reviewer decisions, value signals, friction signals, risk signals, and what remains unknown.

4. What boundary protects the organization?

State allowed inputs, off-limits data, human review gate, rollback trigger, support route, and what the pilot is not allowed to decide.

5. What commitment is requested?

Describe the smallest time, budget attention, tool access, stakeholder meeting, or owner capacity needed for the next reversible step.

6. Who owns the next state?

Name the accountable owner, sponsor, reviewer, support partner, escalation path, and date for the next evidence review.

7. What answer will we accept?

Offer explicit outcomes: approve narrow next step, clarify evidence, reduce scope, pause, stop, or archive. Do not let the meeting end in “interesting.”

Sponsor traps

Prevent these before the ask

Vague ask

“We need support for AI.”

Replace broad support with one decision and one reversible next step. Sponsors can approve decisions; they cannot approve fog.

Proof gap

“It seems promising.”

Use the evidence log and decision memo. If proof is thin, ask for a clarification run instead of a rollout commitment.

Ownership gap

“Who will run it?”

Use the workflow owner card. Sponsor attention without an operating owner turns into orphaned automation.

Safety gap

“What could go wrong?”

Bring the data boundary and risk register. Show the off-limits data, review gate, and stop trigger before asking for capacity.

Adoption gap

“Who has to change?”

Use the adoption change brief to explain affected users, support, training, feedback, and rollback before a sponsor expands the audience.

Decision drift

“Let’s discuss more later.”

End with a named state: approve, clarify, narrow, pause, stop, or archive — plus owner and review date.

Facilitator cue

When to use it in the workshop

After decision memo

Turn evidence into an ask

Use this when the decision memo says continue or clarify and the next step needs sponsor time, owner capacity, or permission to convene people.

Before rollout readiness

Check attention before scale

If the pilot may expand, confirm the sponsor understands the evidence, safety boundary, support path, and reversible commitment.

When buyer conversations stall

Move from interest to decision

Use the brief to convert “we should look at AI” into a small funded experiment, a clarification request, or an honest stop.
