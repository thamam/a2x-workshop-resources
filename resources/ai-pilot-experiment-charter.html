A2X field kit · pilot scoping

Turn an AI pilot idea into one tiny experiment charter.

Use this handout after prioritization, use-case intake, data-boundary work, or tool scoring. It helps a team define the smallest reversible AI pilot experiment: one workflow, one safe sample, one review gate, one evidence question, and one stop condition.

Experiment-charter rule

If the pilot cannot be tested with sanitized input, scoped review, and a reversible next step, it is not ready for an experiment.

Do not use this charter to authorize broad rollout, production automation, customer-facing decisions, sensitive-data access, procurement commitments, pricing claims, or policy exceptions. If the pilot needs those, route the decision to the proper owner before testing. Keep the first experiment narrow enough to stop, rerun, or discard without organizational drama. Very noble. Also: very necessary.

Step 1

Pick the smallest testable workflow slice

Good slice

One repeated task

Choose a workflow moment with a clear before/after artifact: draft, summarize, classify, prepare, review, route, or compare.

Needs narrowing

Too broad

“Improve sales,” “automate support,” or “make operations faster” is not a pilot. Pick one user, one input type, one output, and one review gate.

Stop

Unsafe first test

If the only useful test requires private customer records, credentials, regulated data, production logs, contracts, or irreversible action, pause and approve boundaries first.

Step 2

Write the five experiment boundaries

Hypothesis

State the exact improvement being tested: faster first draft, fewer missed fields, clearer handoff, better review packet, lower support friction, or safer triage.

Allowed input

Name the sanitized, fictional, redacted, or approved sample input. If real data is needed later, make that a separate approval question.

Human review gate

Choose who reviews the output, what evidence they check, and what must be corrected before anyone uses the result.

Success evidence

Pick observable signals such as completion time, missing-field count, reviewer pass/revise/stop notes, support questions, or quality rubric result.

Stop condition

Define the trigger that pauses the experiment: boundary confusion, repeated bad output, unclear ownership, sensitive data need, or reviewer disagreement.

Step 3

Use AI to pressure-test the charter

Decision prompt: “Using only the public-safe pilot charter below, identify what is too broad, unsafe, unreviewable, or unmeasurable. Return: narrowed workflow slice, missing boundary, review gate, success evidence, stop condition, and one first experiment. Do not invent private examples, user claims, pricing, approvals, metrics, or production commitments.”

Copyable worksheet

AI pilot experiment charter

Workflow slice: [one repeated task, one role, one input type, one output].

Hypothesis: [what the AI-assisted step should improve and why it matters].

Allowed input: [toy / fictional / redacted / approved sample; what stays out].

Human review gate: [reviewer role, pass/revise/stop criteria, evidence checked].

Success evidence: [observable signal; no inflated claims or unsupported ROI].

Stop condition: [sensitive data needed / repeated bad output / unclear owner / policy boundary / reviewer disagreement].

First run: [single reversible test, date, owner role, next decision].

Do not include: raw customer data, employee records, production logs, account screenshots, credentials, regulated records, vendor contracts, pricing claims, private repository links, or internal strategy.

When to use this

Good moments for the handout

After use-case intake

Convert a promising idea into the smallest experiment before anyone asks for tooling, access, or rollout.

Before evidence logging

Make sure the evidence log will record a real test, not a vague enthusiasm packet.

Before sponsor updates

Show that the team can test safely with boundaries, review, and stop conditions before asking for bigger commitment.
