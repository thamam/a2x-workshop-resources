Use this one-page memo after a small AI workflow trial. It helps a team decide whether to continue, clarify, or stop based on evidence from a safe pilot—not vendor hype, executive pressure, or demo-day excitement.

Safe memo rule

Write the memo from observed outputs, not private data.

Keep the memo public-safe by naming the workflow and evidence pattern without pasting secrets, customer material, private repository links, production screenshots, billing pages, tokens, or internal strategy. If the trial required sensitive access, the recommendation should be “clarify first” until a safer evaluation path exists.

Memo structure

Six fields are enough

1. Decision ask

What should the team approve now: continue one more week, run a narrower test, assign an owner, or stop?

2. Workflow tested

Name the repeated job, intended user, safe input, and what was explicitly out of scope.

3. Evidence observed

List visible artifacts: accepted draft, cleaner spec, reduced review loops, working prototype, useful summary, or failed setup signal.

4. Risks and boundaries

Record what must stay guarded: private data, credentials, external messages, production changes, compliance review, procurement, or team capacity.

5. Adoption requirement

State what must exist before scaling: project rules, reusable skill, review checklist, owner, training note, evaluation fixture, or approval gate.

6. Next reversible step

Choose the smallest next action that creates better evidence without committing the organization too early.

Decision options

Use a narrow recommendation

Continue

The pilot produced a useful artifact, stayed inside safety boundaries, and has a clear owner plus a visible next verification step.

Clarify first

The idea is promising, but workflow fit, access, evaluation evidence, policy, or adoption ownership is still too vague.

Stop or park

The trial needs sensitive access too early, produced only vibes, duplicates existing workflow, lacks a user, or cannot be verified safely.

Facilitator cue

When to use it in the workshop

After scoring

Close the loop

Use this immediately after the AI tool trial scorecard so “run now” becomes an accountable pilot rather than a vague experiment.

After a demo

Separate excitement from evidence

Ask what the demo proved, what it did not prove, and what would have to be true before a real team adopts it.

Before scaling

Keep the next step reversible

If the next step needs sensitive data, production access, pricing commitment, or external messages, stop and design a safer test first.
