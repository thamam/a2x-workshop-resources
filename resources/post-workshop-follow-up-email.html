Follow-up rule

Make the email helpful without turning it into lead capture or private-data collection.

Do not include confidential attendee lists, customer names, raw workshop notes, private repository links, credentials, pricing promises, analytics tags, tracking links, or claims about guaranteed outcomes. Point people to public-safe resources and ask them to choose a reversible next step with safe sample input.

Email ingredients

Keep the message simple enough to act on

1 · Recap
Name what changed

Remind attendees of the workshop frame: agents need rules, safe inputs, review gates, and visible verification evidence.

2 · One path
Choose the first next resource

Recommend the action plan, handoff packet, route chooser, or prompt safety preflight instead of linking everything at once.

3 · Boundary
Say what not to paste

Warn against secrets, customer data, employee records, raw transcripts, production logs, contracts, account screenshots, credentials, and private repositories.

4 · Tiny commitment
Ask for a reversible next move

Invite the team to pick one repeated workflow, one safe sample, one owner, one reviewer, and one evidence signal.

5 · Escalation
Respect approval boundaries

If the next step requires sensitive data, a new tool, procurement, legal/security review, or production changes, route it through the approved internal process.

6 · No automation creep
Do not imply tracking or enrollment

This template is a human-written follow-up aid. It does not add forms, analytics, unsubscribe handling, CRM actions, or mass-mail workflow.

Post-workshop follow-up email

Subject: Claude Code workshop follow-up: pick one safe next workflow Hi [team/name], Thanks for joining the workshop. The main takeaway was not “use more AI everywhere.” It was: give the agent a safe target, clear rules, human review, and visible evidence before expanding the workflow. Suggested first move: 1. Open the post-workshop action plan: [public-safe hub link]. 2. Pick one repeated low-risk workflow. 3. Use a toy, fictional, redacted, or approved sample input. 4. Name the human reviewer and stop condition. 5. Decide what evidence would prove the next run worked. Please do not paste secrets, customer data, employee records, raw transcripts, production logs, contracts, account screenshots, credentials, private repository contents, pricing material, or regulated records into shared AI tools unless your approved tool and policy explicitly allow it. If the next step needs a new tool, sensitive data, procurement/legal/security review, production-system access, or customer-facing claims, pause and route it through your normal approval process first. Useful starting links: - Resource chooser: [public-safe hub link] - Post-workshop action plan: [public-safe hub link] - Claude Code handoff packet: [public-safe hub link] - Prompt safety preflight: [public-safe hub link] A good first follow-up is small: one workflow, one safe sample, one owner, one reviewer, one evidence signal. Best, [Sender]
Variants

Choose the version that fits the room

Short

For busy attendees

Send only the subject, one-sentence recap, action-plan link, and do-not-paste boundary. Use this when people already know the next workflow.

Manager

For team leads

Add a request for one owner, reviewer, and evidence signal. Avoid asking for private examples in the reply thread.

Blocked

For sensitive next steps

Replace the action-plan ask with a policy exception, procurement, legal/security, or sponsor-alignment route before any real data or production work begins.

Quality check

Before sending, remove anything that turns this into a risky blast

Link check

Use public-safe hub links only. Do not include private repository URLs, local file paths, account pages, or internal docs unless the audience and approval boundary are explicit.

Promise check

Do not claim guaranteed productivity, cost savings, compliance, security, or adoption outcomes. Keep the message grounded in practice, review, and evidence.

Reply check

If you ask for replies, ask for generic workflow categories and owner roles — not customer data, employee details, raw notes, tickets, logs, or credentials.
