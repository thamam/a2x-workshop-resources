Safe prioritization rule

Rank patterns, not private examples.

Describe each candidate generically: role, repeated workflow, safe sample input, reviewable output, and decision evidence. Do not paste customer data, employee records, production logs, account screenshots, private repository links, credentials, vendor contracts, regulated records, or internal strategy into a shared prioritization doc.

Step 1

Collect three to five candidates

Repeated work

Does it happen often enough?

Look for weekly or daily work with a clear trigger, not one-off strategy questions or novelty demos.

Reviewable output

Can a human judge it quickly?

Prefer drafts, summaries, checklists, diffs, routing suggestions, or decision packets that a reviewer can accept, edit, or reject.

Safe sample

Can it be tested without sensitive data?

Use fictional customers, toy repos, sanitized notes, copied non-sensitive docs, or a tiny synthetic dataset.

Boundary

What must stay out?

Exclude external sending, production writes, payments, private data, regulated records, security-sensitive decisions, and irreversible actions unless explicitly approved.

Step 2

Score each candidate 1–5

Give each row a simple score. A boring 4 with safe data and a clear reviewer beats a flashy 5 that needs private access, vague ownership, or unreviewable output.

1. Workflow pull

1: occasional curiosity. 3: recurring pain for one team. 5: repeated workflow with clear demand, bottleneck, or review burden.

2. Safe sample availability

1: needs sensitive data now. 3: can be partially sanitized. 5: can be tested with toy, fictional, or copied non-sensitive material.

3. Reviewability

1: output is hard to verify. 3: review is possible but fuzzy. 5: reviewer can apply a checklist, diff, acceptance criteria, or visible pass/fail signal.

4. Reversibility

1: touches production, customers, money, or policy. 3: reversible with care. 5: local, draft-only, approval-gated, and easy to abandon.

5. Owner capacity

1: no accountable owner. 3: owner exists but review time is uncertain. 5: owner, backup, reviewer, and review cadence are named.

Step 3

Choose the next state

Total 20–25: define the use case

Move to the AI use-case intake brief. Keep the scope tiny, use sanitized input, and name the review gate before tool scoring.

Total 14–19: clarify first

Improve the candidate by shrinking scope, finding a safer sample, naming the reviewer, or choosing a more visible success signal.

Total under 14: park it

Do not spend workshop energy here yet. Park the candidate until boundaries, ownership, reviewability, or sample safety improve.

Copyable prioritization note

Paste this into your planning doc

Candidate workflow: [role] repeatedly turns [safe input] into [reviewable output] for [decision/handoff].

Workflow pull: [1–5] because [frequency, bottleneck, or review burden].

Safe sample availability: [1–5] using [toy repo / sanitized notes / fictional customer / copied non-sensitive docs].

Reviewability: [1–5] checked by [reviewer + checklist/diff/acceptance signal].

Reversibility: [1–5] with [draft-only boundary, approval gate, rollback, or stop condition].

Owner capacity: [1–5] owned by [owner], reviewed by [reviewer], next review on [date].

Total and next state: [score] → define use case / clarify first / park it.

Off-limits: secrets, customer data, employee records, production systems, private links, external sends, payments, pricing claims, and irreversible actions.
