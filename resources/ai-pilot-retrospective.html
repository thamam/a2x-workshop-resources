Safe retrospective rule

Record lessons without replaying sensitive work.

Describe the workflow, evidence type, decision signal, policy implication, and next safe action. Do not paste customer data, employee details, private repositories, credentials, production screenshots, account pages, pricing, vendor contracts, or internal strategy into a broadly shared retrospective.

Retrospective structure

Answer seven short questions

1. What was the pilot trying to prove?

Name the workflow, user role, starting hypothesis, and smallest success signal.

2. What evidence actually appeared?

Use sanitized artifacts, review notes, quality comparisons, time observations, adoption signals, or failure examples without exposing private content.

3. What worked repeatably?

List the prompt pattern, project rule, review gate, data boundary, or handoff habit that should become standard.

4. What failed or stayed fragile?

Name the failure mode: weak inputs, missing owner, unsafe access request, inconsistent output, poor verification, or unclear policy.

5. Which guardrail changes now?

Decide whether to update project rules, approval gates, review steps, risk owners, sample-data policy, or stop conditions.

6. What is the decision?

Choose continue, narrow, pause, stop, or graduate to a controlled rollout only if the evidence supports it.

7. What is the next experiment?

Define one reversible next step with owner, sanitized input, review date, and evidence threshold.

Decision outcomes

Pick the honest next state

Continue

Same scope, better evidence

The pilot was useful and safe, but the next step is still a controlled test with clearer fixtures, reviewers, or acceptance signals.

Narrow

Promising, but too broad

The value is real in one lane, while other workflows, data types, teams, or access patterns remain out of scope.

Pause or stop

Risk or value mismatch

The pilot exposed missing ownership, weak evidence, policy uncertainty, unsafe access needs, or insufficient workflow fit.

Facilitator cue

When to use it in the workshop

After the update

Close the loop

Use the retrospective after stakeholders have seen status, risk, and asks so the team can decide what to keep, change, or stop.

Before rollout talk

Prevent premature scaling

Require lessons and guardrail updates before expanding to real customers, production actions, sensitive data, or more teams.

For the next pilot

Reuse what was learned

Convert strong lessons into project rules, risk-register defaults, or a better trial scorecard before the next experiment starts.
