Safe register rule

Describe the risk pattern, not the sensitive example.

Keep the register safe to share by naming categories and controls without pasting customer data, private repository links, production screenshots, access tokens, billing details, vendor pricing, internal strategy, or personal account information. If a risk cannot be discussed without secrets, mark it as “needs private review” and keep the public worksheet generic.

Register structure

Fill one row per risk

1. Risk category

Choose the plain-language bucket: data exposure, wrong output, unauthorized action, adoption failure, tool reliability, vendor dependency, policy mismatch, or unclear ownership.

2. Where it appears

Name the workflow step, user role, handoff, or decision point where the risk could show up.

3. Boundary

State what the pilot must not touch yet: real customers, production systems, external messages, credentials, billing decisions, regulated data, or irreversible changes.

4. Mitigation

Pick the smallest control: sanitized fixture, human review, read-only mode, approval gate, rollback path, logging note, owner check, or narrower scope.

5. Owner and review signal

Assign a person or role, then define the visible evidence that says the risk is acceptable, needs more work, or should stop the pilot.

6. Stop condition

Write the exact signal that forces the team to pause before continuing, such as repeated hallucinated outputs, policy uncertainty, unsafe data need, or no adoption owner.

Common risk buckets

Start with the obvious ones

Sensitive input creep

The pilot starts with sanitized examples but needs real customer, employee, source, or financial data before the control plan is ready.

Action without review

The workflow can draft, edit, send, delete, approve, or deploy something before a human has checked the output and context.

No evidence path

The team likes the demo but cannot name the artifact, quality bar, comparison baseline, or acceptance signal that proves value.

Owner gap

Nobody is clearly responsible for maintaining prompts, rules, access, review habits, training notes, or rollback decisions.

Safe enough to continue

The next step uses copied non-sensitive inputs, has a review gate, has a named owner, and can be stopped without operational damage.

Facilitator cue

When to use it in the workshop

After the memo

Turn “continue” into guardrails

If the pilot decision memo says continue, require at least one risk row before adding scope, users, integrations, or sensitive access.

Before real data

Protect the boundary

When the next useful test wants private data or production access, pause and define the control, owner, and stop condition first.

During review

Separate fixable risk from stop risk

Some risks need a better prompt or review checklist. Others need policy, procurement, security, or leadership review before the pilot expands.
