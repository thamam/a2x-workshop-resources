Decision-log rule

One decision, one owner, one revisit trigger.

A steering decision log is not a transcript. It is a compact record of the state change and the evidence boundary. Keep it sanitized: do not paste customer data, employee records, contracts, pricing promises, production credentials, account screenshots, internal strategy, raw tickets, regulated records, private repository links, or unredacted incident details into shared decision notes.

What to capture

Fill seven fields before the meeting disperses

1. Decision date and forum

Name the steering cadence, sponsor review, health review, retrospective, or decision meeting that produced the decision.

2. Decision type

Choose the state: continue, clarify, narrow, expand, pause, stop, archive, escalate, change guardrail, or request more evidence.

3. Workflow boundary

Record what users, inputs, outputs, tools, data categories, and review gates are allowed after the decision — and what remains off-limits.

4. Evidence basis

Summarize sanitized evidence categories: usage pattern, value signal, quality signal, reviewer decisions, support friction, incident pattern, or owner capacity.

5. Owner and next action

Assign one accountable owner, one next action, one due date, and the artifact that must be updated before the next review.

6. Reversal or revisit condition

Name the signal that would reverse, pause, narrow, or escalate the decision: incident, quality drop, support overload, weak evidence, boundary drift, or missing owner time.

7. Communication note

Write the short sanitized message that stakeholders, users, or reviewers need so the decision does not become rumor or accidental permission.

Decision states

Use precise language instead of vibes

Continue

Keep the current boundary.

Evidence is good enough for another cycle, with the same allowed users, inputs, review gate, and owner.

Clarify

Hold expansion while one question is answered.

Use when evidence is promising but a data boundary, owner, reviewer, support path, or success signal is still ambiguous.

Narrow

Reduce scope before risk spreads.

Use when the workflow is useful but too broad: fewer users, safer inputs, clearer review gate, or a smaller automation step.

Expand narrowly

Let the next group in deliberately.

Only expand with named users, allowed inputs, rollback owner, support route, and a date for the next evidence review.

Pause or stop

Protect trust before momentum wins.

Use when evidence, safety, ownership, support, or stakeholder confidence is not strong enough to continue responsibly.

Escalate

Move a decision to the right authority.

Escalate budget, policy, regulated data, security, legal, or production-impact questions instead of burying them in a pilot note.

Facilitator cue

When to use it in the workshop

After steering cadence

Turn discussion into operating memory

Use it when the cadence changes a boundary, artifact, owner, threshold, or review date.

After health review

Make the next review testable

Use it when a workflow stays active but needs a specific improvement, watch signal, or reversal condition.

Before expansion

Prevent accidental permission

Use it before more users, inputs, teams, or automations enter the AI workflow so the expansion stays narrow and reversible.
