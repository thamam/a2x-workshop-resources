Value-scorecard rule

Score evidence categories, not excitement.

A value scorecard is not a revenue model, pricing claim, or business case promise. Keep it grounded in sanitized observations: workflow frequency, user friction, review effort, quality signal, support burden, risk movement, and owner capacity. Do not paste customer data, employee records, contracts, pricing promises, production credentials, account screenshots, internal strategy, raw tickets, regulated records, private repository links, or unredacted incident details into shared scorecards.

Score dimensions

Use five signals before deciding what comes next

1. Workflow pull

Is this a real repeated workflow with clear users, or only a shiny demo? Score observed demand, frequency, and whether users return without being pushed.

2. Quality and review signal

Compare sanitized outputs against the human review gate. Score whether the agent/tool improves first drafts, reduces rework, or creates review debt.

3. Friction and support load

Record setup issues, prompt confusion, account problems, reviewer time, support questions, and failure recovery. A useful pilot can still be too heavy to operate.

4. Safety and boundary stability

Check whether allowed inputs, review gates, retention, escalation, and off-limits categories held during real use. Boundary drift lowers the score even when outputs look good.

5. Ownership and next-cycle capacity

Name who will own the next cycle, what artifact they update, and what evidence they can realistically collect before the next steering or health review.

Decision thresholds

Translate the score into a narrow next state

Continue

Evidence supports another cycle.

Use when pull, quality, safety, and ownership are all adequate inside the current boundary. Keep the scope unchanged and set the next review date.

Clarify

One signal is still ambiguous.

Use when the workflow seems promising but a data boundary, reviewer rule, owner, support path, or measurement signal needs a tighter answer.

Narrow

Value exists, but the scope is too wide.

Use when the pilot works for one input type, user group, or step but should not spread until the weakest boundary is repaired.

Expand narrowly

The next group can join deliberately.

Only expand with named users, allowed inputs, a rollback path, support owner, and the exact evidence that would reverse the decision.

Pause or stop

Trust beats sunk-cost momentum.

Use when quality, safety, ownership, support, or user pull is not strong enough to justify another active cycle.

Escalate

The next decision needs authority.

Escalate policy, security, regulated data, budget attention, vendor commitment, or production-impact questions instead of hiding them in a score.

After evidence log

Before sponsor ask

Use it before sponsor alignment so the ask is tied to observed workflow proof, not generic AI optimism.
